Why net farm income is an incomplete yardstick for the farm safety net

The question this package answers

How well is the farm safety net working? In U.S. policy discussion the de facto answer is annual net farm income (NFI), the headline sector aggregate that USDA’s Economic Research Service forecasts and that commentators track to judge the “health” of the farm economy and, by extension, the programs that support it (USDA-ERS, n.d.-a). Yet the stated purpose of those programs is not to raise the average so much as to manage risk, that is, to dampen the year-to-year variability of farm outcomes (Glauber, 2004; Coble & Barnett, 2013; Hardaker, Lien, Anderson, & Huirne, 2015).

NFI is indispensable for what it was designed to do: summarize the sector’s income in a year. But as a leading indicator for the safety net it is incomplete, because it does not measure what the safety net is for, and as a single aggregate it obscures how outcomes are distributed across heterogeneous farms (Mishra, El-Osta, Morehart, Johnson, & Hopkins, 2002). This vignette lays out the complementary indicator that fills that gap. It draws on a connected research program: farm-level evaluation of risk-reducing instruments (Tsiboe, Tack, & Yu, 2023); large-scale calibration of the farm-level yields needed to do so (Tsiboe, Turner, & Yu, 2025); the risk-reduction metrics themselves (Tsiboe et al., 2025); the evaluation of program combinations (Gaku & Tsiboe, 2025); and prospective assessment of proposed instruments (Tsiboe & Turner, 2025). The final section shows how rfsnEfficiency operationalizes it.

What is a farm safety-net instrument?

A farm safety-net instrument is any program or contract that moves income to a producer in adverse states of the world, conditioned on a yield loss, a price or revenue shortfall, or a disaster, in order to stabilize farm outcomes (Glauber, 2004; Hardaker et al., 2015). Operationally, an instrument is defined by four primitives:

  1. a trigger: the adverse event that activates a payment (a farm yield below a guarantee, a county revenue below a benchmark, a declared disaster);
  2. an indemnity / payment rule: how the payment maps to the shortfall;
  3. a producer cost: a (often subsidized) premium or fee, or none; and
  4. a risk scope: the pool over which losses are recognized, namely farm-level, county / area, or national.

The scope primitive is consequential, not cosmetic. Area- and index-based triggers economize on information but introduce basis risk, the wedge between the index and the farm’s own outcome, which can sharply limit (or even reverse) an instrument’s farm-level risk reduction (Tsiboe et al., 2023). These four primitives are exactly the inputs rfsnEfficiency takes (outcome draws, plus an indemnity rule that may be a function, plus cost / premium / subsidy), so any instrument, existing or proposed, can be scored on the same footing.

The U.S. farm safety net: current scope

Using the United States as context, federal programs span three groups (see the USFarmSafetyNetLab; CRS, 2021; Glauber, 2004):

  1. Farm-specific risk management: the Federal Crop Insurance Program (FCIP) and the Noninsured Crop Disaster Assistance Program (NAP), administered by USDA’s Risk Management Agency (USDA-RMA, n.d.). Producers pay (subsidized) premiums; triggers are farm- or area-level yield/revenue outcomes. FCIP is the largest instrument and is itself layered (basic yield and revenue plans, unit structures, coverage levels, options, and supplemental endorsements such as SCO and ECO), and its premium subsidies are large enough that participation and design are shaped as much by the subsidy as by risk (Coble & Barnett, 2013; Du, Feng, & Hennessy, 2017; ARPC, n.d.).
  2. Regional or national risk coverage: Price Loss Coverage (PLC) and Agriculture Risk Coverage (ARC) under Title I, alongside marketing-loan and standing disaster programs. Producers pay no premium; triggers are national or county price/revenue benchmarks.
  3. Ad-hoc disaster assistance: temporary, premium-free programs responding to events not well covered by the first two groups, for example pandemic- or trade-related assistance and large disaster packages.

Because a producer typically holds a combination across these groups (say, a revenue FCIP policy plus PLC and, in a shock year, ad-hoc aid), the safety net’s performance is a property of the combination, not of any single instrument. That is the central insight of combination-strategy evaluation (Gaku & Tsiboe, 2025). The menu is also not fixed: proposed instruments, such as a buy-up tier for PLC, can be assessed before enactment for the risk reduction they would deliver and at what cost (Tsiboe & Turner, 2025). Either way, evaluation requires scoring configurable instruments on the same realized draws.

Why NFI is an incomplete yardstick for the safety net

A risk-management program exists to reduce the variability of farm outcomes, to convert volatile, loss-prone years into something steadier, not merely to raise the average (Hardaker et al., 2015). Judging it by NFI alone leaves four gaps:

  1. It targets the level, not the risk. NFI tracks the mean. A program could leave mean income unchanged yet sharply cut its year-to-year swings, exactly the success a safety net is meant to deliver, and NFI would not move. Risk, moreover, is asymmetric: producers care chiefly about downside shortfalls below a target, which mean-level statistics ignore (Fishburn, 1977; Hardaker et al., 2015).
  2. It is an aggregate. A single national figure hides the distribution, namely which crops, regions, and operations actually had their risk reduced and which did not, a long-standing limitation of sector-level income measures (Mishra et al., 2002). Farm-level evaluation is required to recover it (Tsiboe et al., 2023).
  3. It is partly circular. NFI includes government payments (USDA-ERS, n.d.-b), so a more expensive program mechanically raises the very number used to judge it, regardless of whether it stabilized anything; this is sharpened by the fact that subsidies function in part as income transfers rather than pure risk protection (Coble & Barnett, 2013; Du et al., 2017).
  4. It is ex post and confounded. Realized NFI mixes program effects with prices, weather, and macro conditions, so it cannot isolate the marginal contribution of a given configuration. Disentangling it requires a counterfactual, representative-agent comparison against a no-program baseline, which is feasible only with farm-level calibrated outcomes (Tsiboe, Turner, & Yu, 2025; Tsiboe et al., 2025).

The alternative: efficacy and efficiency of risk reduction

Tsiboe et al. (2025) formalize the safety net’s job as a comparison between the distribution of a farm outcome with the program and the counterfactual without it (the no-program baseline). Two questions follow.

Efficacy, or does it work? Two indices, each relative to the no-program baseline (so 1 = no effect):

  • Income Transfer Score (ITS): the ratio of mean outcome with vs. without the program. ITS > 1 means the program lifts the mean.
  • Variability Reduction Score (VRS): the ratio of an outcome-variability measure with vs. without the program. VRS < 1 means it cuts variability. Because downside risk is what producers price, the package reports VRS not only on the coefficient of variation but also on loss-side (below-mean) partial moments (Fishburn, 1977).

A configuration “reduces risk” only when it does both: ITS > 1 and VRS < 1.

Efficiency, or is it worth it? The Risk-Reduction Efficiency Ratio (RRER), Equation (3) of Tsiboe et al. (2025):

\[ \text{RRER} \;=\; \mathbb{I}(\text{VRS}<1)\,\mathbb{1}(\text{ITS}>1)\; \frac{1-\text{VRS}}{\text{ITS}-1}. \]

RRER is the variability reduction achieved per unit of income (outcome) transferred. It deflates the stabilization a program delivers by how much it had to move the mean to get there. A program that simply hands out money every year raises the mean a lot for little genuine stabilization and earns a low RRER; a program that converts each transferred dollar into a large reduction in volatility earns a high one. The same per-dollar logic underlies the cost-effectiveness comparison of a proposed PLC buy-up tier in Tsiboe & Turner (2025). Crucially, RRER is defined only when the configuration is already efficacious: efficiency presumes efficacy.

Keeping the indices well-posed: economic bounds

ITS, VRS, and RRER are all ratios, so an agent whose no-program baseline has a mean or variability near zero can push an index to an implausible magnitude that reflects a vanishing denominator rather than any economic effect. Across a Monte-Carlo of \(\sim\!10^{5}\) heterogeneous agents, a handful of such degenerate cases would otherwise dominate the weighted or trimmed aggregates. To keep the indicators well-posed, rfsnEfficiency winsorizes each index into an economically feasible envelope (arguments its_clamp, vrs_clamp, and the RRER deadband / cap), pulling the degenerate agents to the edge of the plausible range instead of letting them set the headline. The defaults, and the economic priors behind them, are:

Index Bound Default Economic reason
ITS (mean_index) floor 0.10 Subsidized insurance cannot collapse mean revenue
ITS (mean_index) cap 2.00 Transfers are a modest share of revenue; the mean cannot double
VRS (cv_index, downside, shortfall) floor 0.10 Partial coverage and basis risk leave irreducible residual risk
VRS (cv_index, downside, shortfall) cap 2.00 Insurance may add noise, but not multiply variability
RRER (efficiency) floor 0.00 Zero unless the program both lifts the mean and cuts risk (definitional)
RRER (efficiency) max \(1/\text{deadband}\approx 18\text{–}20\) Efficiency is undefined near a zero income transfer

Index by index:

  1. ITS floor (0.10). Participation in a subsidized program is rational only when the expected net benefit is non-negative, so premium subsidies roughly offset the loading and \(\mathbb{E}[\text{indemnity}+\text{subsidy}]\ge\) the producer premium (Coble & Barnett, 2013; Du et al., 2017). A functioning program therefore lifts, or at worst barely dents, mean revenue; an ITS of 0.10 would mean it destroyed 90% of expected revenue, which can only arise from a baseline mean near zero.
  2. ITS cap (2.00). Premiums are single-digit percentages of liability and subsidies cover roughly 60% of premium, so the mean uplift is on the order of a few percent. Because indemnities are bounded by liability (\(\approx\) coverage \(\times\) expected revenue), the mean simply cannot double; \(\text{ITS}\gg 1.5\) signals a micro-baseline artifact.
  3. VRS floor (0.10). Coverage tops out at \(\le 90\%\), deductibles remain, and area endorsements (SCO/ECO) carry basis risk between the county pool and the farm (Tsiboe et al., 2023). That irreducible residual means variability cannot be driven to zero; the floor (“at most \(\sim\!90\%\) of variability removed”) is consistent with partial coverage and basis risk.
  4. VRS cap (2.00). Insurance can add variance in small doses (premium outflow in good years; area basis risk), so mild \(\text{VRS}>1\) is admitted, but doubling revenue variability is already extreme and larger values are tiny-baseline artifacts.
  5. RRER floor (0). By Equation (3) the score is zero unless the configuration both lifts the mean and cuts variability, so non-negativity is built in, not imposed.
  6. RRER deadband (0.05). RRER divides the proportional risk reduction by the proportional income gain, so as the mean gain \(\text{ITS}-1\to 0\) the ratio explodes: a program that stabilizes with essentially no transfer looks infinitely efficient. Economically, a mean gain below \(\sim\!5\%\) is within the noise of the transfer mechanism and is not a meaningful cost basis to divide by; requiring \(\text{ITS}-1\ge 0.05\) before rating efficiency removes that singularity and mechanically bounds the score at \(1/0.05=20\) (about 18 given the VRS floor).

These guardrails complement, rather than replace, the robust aggregation the package already uses: the clamps fix per-agent artifacts at the source, trimmed means with medians reported alongside handle the group aggregation, and the ITS deadband removes the one remaining singularity (RRER as \(\text{ITS}\to 1\)). All are optional; set the argument to NULL (or deadband = 0) to recover the raw Equation (3) behaviour.

What risk-reduction efficiency adds

Property Annual NFI Risk-Reduction Efficiency
Measures risk (variability) No (level only) Yes
Captures downside / shortfall risk No Yes (Fishburn, 1977)
Distributional (by crop/region/agent) No (aggregate) Yes (Tsiboe et al., 2023)
Isolates the program’s marginal effect No (confounded) Yes (vs. baseline)
Compares alternative configurations No Yes (Gaku & Tsiboe, 2025)
Independent of program spending No (includes payments) Yes (spending is the denominator, by design)
Forward / counterfactual capable No (ex post) Yes (Tsiboe & Turner, 2025)

In short, RRER reports how much risk reduction the safety net buys per dollar it moves, agent by agent. That is precisely the question a risk-management program should be judged on, and precisely the dimension NFI leaves out. It does not displace NFI; it completes the picture.

How rfsnEfficiency operationalizes it

The package is program-agnostic: you supply each agent’s end-of-season outcome draws and the program parameters (an indemnity rule that may be a function, plus cost/premium/subsidy), and it returns the scores and regime comparisons. The outcome draws can come from calibrated farm-level yields of the kind developed in Tsiboe, Turner, & Yu (2025).

library(rfsnEfficiency)
data(toy_outcomes)   # built from the calibrated_yield release; see data-raw/

# Score a configuration and compare two regimes against the same draws
res <- run_efficiency_analysis(
  data = toy_outcomes, by = "group_id",
  outcome_args = list(base_value = "outcome", transfers = "indemnity",
                      premium = "premium"),
  ref = "baseline", alt = "reform")

res$scores   # ITS (mean_index), VRS (risk_index), RRER (efficiency) per regime
res$deltas   # reform - baseline for every score

Because the outcome is configurable (revenue, profit, margin, or a cost-side measure), the same indicator can be reported on whichever farm outcome a policy audience cares about, without changing the metric.

What NFI still does well

This is not an argument to discard NFI. As a macro accounting aggregate it remains the right tool for tracking sector income and budget exposure (USDA-ERS, n.d.-a). The claim is narrower: NFI is an incomplete yardstick for the safety net, and a risk-reduction-efficiency indicator supplies the missing piece, a direct, distributional measure of the safety net’s core function, reducing farm-level risk efficiently. It can be computed retrospectively as a report card and prospectively to compare reforms before they are enacted, on the same realized shocks (Gaku & Tsiboe, 2025; Tsiboe & Turner, 2025; Tsiboe et al., 2025).
